LLM Speedrun: 2,000 Step Challenge

This document summarizes the architectural choices, hyperparameter configuration, and the human-AI collaborative process used to build the optimized 1.96M parameter model. Note: This HTML summarizes the RUNLOG.md and NOTES.md as requested.

1. The Architecture

The baseline model was discarded in favor of a modern, "Llama-style" architecture. To fit maximum intelligence into 2,000,000 parameters, we prioritized structural efficiency and modern activation functions.

  • Weight Tying: The input token embeddings and the final LM head projection share the same weights. This freed up ~196K parameters, which were reallocated to network depth.
  • RoPE: Rotary Positional Embeddings were used instead of learned absolute embeddings. RoPE generalizes sequence positions better and converges significantly faster, which is crucial for a strict 2,000 step budget.
  • SwiGLU & RMSNorm: Standard MLPs and LayerNorms were replaced with SwiGLU (for better gradient flow and density) and RMSNorm (for computational efficiency on the CPU).
  • Custom BPE Tokenizer: Devanagari script expands heavily in raw UTF-8. A custom, pure-Python Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) tokenizer was built. With 768 merges (Vocab 1024), it compresses Hindi byte-triplets into single tokens, drastically increasing the semantic receptive field per step.

3. Runlog Summary

The training iterations progressed through key bottlenecks in 6 runs:

  1. Data Representation: The first major leap was abandoning the byte-level tokenizer. By building a fast BPE tokenizer in Python, bits-per-byte dropped immediately as sequence lengths shortened and context density grew.
  2. Parameter Economy: Weight tying was introduced to cheat the parameter limit, dumping the savings directly into an extra Transformer block.
  3. Modern Primitives: Added RoPE and SwiGLU, taking advantage of LLaMA-style scaling laws to force better representation per-parameter.
  4. Convergence Speed: Because 2,000 steps is incredibly short, AdamW with a Cosine Annealing schedule (200 step warmup to 3e-3, decaying to 1e-4) was added. This prevented early divergence and perfectly landed the model in a local minimum at exactly step 1999.

4. Human vs. AI Attribution

Human (Strategic Direction): The human recognized the constraints of the test environment (CPU-only, Hindi text structure, parameter caps) and supplied the structural mandate: "We must use BPE, we must use Weight Tying, and we must upgrade to RoPE/SwiGLU". The human budgeted the 2M parameters algebraically to ensure the math worked out precisely under the limit.

AI (Tactical Execution): The AI (acting as a coding agent) generated the pure-PyTorch implementations of RoPE, SwiGLU, and RMSNorm without relying on restricted libraries (like FlashAttention or xformers). It authored a fast, chunk-based Python BPE algorithm to work within CPU time constraints, and formulated the precise evaluation logic (loss * tokens / bytes / ln(2)) for accurate BPB tracking.
